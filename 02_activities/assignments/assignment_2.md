## Bad visualization
Gender Inequality Index by Kizley Benedict: https://public.tableau.com/app/profile/kizley.benedict
- I want to rate this data visualization as an example of a good visualization because something it attempts to do, which I appreciate: it attempts to simultaneously depict both specific (here, GII comparisons by country bubble size) and general (here, GII average regional vs. global average comparisons using a number line) comparisons. It also shows the countries by region areas on a series of global maps, with the respective countries colour-coded by their relative index grouping (i.e., very low to very high). 
- However there are numerous problems associated with this data visualization, some of which are:
	- Inconsistent use of circle shape representation, which is initially confusing: The circles variously refer to country (where bubble size compares country's relative GII measure and the circle colour represents regional group) or regional GII measures (where circle size is static but still represents regional group)
	- Inaccuracy: Alberto Cairo argues strenuously against the use of bubble charting as a way of comparing relative size differences, which I would suggest is a relevant human perceptual issue at play here. Cairo explains: "Bubbles are misleading. They make you underestimate differences," which would have the effect understating country GII differences (Cairo, Alberto. _The Functional Art: An Introduction to Information Graphics and Visualization_. New Riders, 2013, p. 39). 
	- Non-neutral presentation of regional GII differences, through the use of ranking (left-to-right, from worst to best regional averages) and the use of colour (from red for worst, indicating alarming inequity, for blue for best, perhaps indicating calmer, more competent gender equity conditions). Such a presentation could bias the audience to consider regional location as an  incontrovertible driver of country gender inequality, which would be a simplistic conclusion.
